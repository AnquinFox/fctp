# Xiamen & Gulangyu: Island Life at Its Finest

So, Xiamen. I'll be honest — before I went, I had this vague image of a sleepy coastal town with nice weather and maybe a few old buildings. What I actually found was so much more. This city has this ridiculously relaxed energy that hits you the moment you step off the train. The air smells like sea salt and jasmine, everyone's on a scooter or a bicycle, and somehow the pace of life just... slows down. It's the kind of place where you plan to do five things in a day and end up doing two, and somehow that feels totally fine.

Let me walk you through what I discovered — organized by area so you can plan your own lazy adventure.

## Gulangyu Island — The Car-Free Paradise

This is the one everyone talks about, and for good reason. Gulangyu (鼓浪屿) is a tiny island just a 5-minute ferry ride from downtown Xiamen, and here's the kicker — **no cars are allowed**. No scooters, no taxis, nothing with an engine except the occasional electric buggy for supplies. Your legs are your only transport, and honestly? That's the magic of it.

The whole island is this maze of winding lanes lined with colonial-era villas, overgrown bougainvillea, and random cats napping on garden walls. Some of the buildings are gorgeous — a weird but wonderful mix of European neoclassical, Southern Fujianese, and Art Deco that makes you feel like you're wandering through a Southeast Asian version of a Mediterranean island.

Don't try to "optimize" your route. Just get lost. Every alley leads to something interesting — a random piano museum, a tiny bakery selling freshly baked coconut cakes, a viewpoint overlooking the Xiamen skyline. That's the whole point.

### Sunlight Rock

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | 62 Huangyan Rd, Gulangyu Island |
| **Getting There** | 15-min walk from the ferry terminal, uphill |
| **Hours** | 7:00 AM — 6:00 PM (winter) / 7:00 AM — 7:00 PM (summer) |
| **Fee** | ¥50 / ~$7 |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#viewpoint` `#photography` `#moderate_hike`

The highest point on the island, and the only place where you really feel like you're above the treetops. The climb is about 10 minutes of stairs — not bad at all — and the payoff is a 360° panorama of the island, the Xiamen skyline across the water, and the sea stretching out to the horizon. I went at 4 PM on a clear day and the light was absurdly beautiful. Worth the entry fee just for that view.

### Shuzhuang Garden

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | 7 Gangzaihou Rd, Gulangyu Island |
| **Getting There** | 10-min walk southeast from ferry terminal |
| **Hours** | 7:00 AM — 6:00 PM |
| **Fee** | ¥30 / ~$4 (combo with Piano Museum available) |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#garden` `#history` `#photography`

This is a seaside garden built in 1913 by a Taiwanese businessman, and it's exactly the kind of place where you'd want to sit and read a book for an afternoon. It's small but cleverly designed — winding paths, rockeries, a little bridge, and the sea right there on one side. The best bit is the **Piano Museum** inside (yes, Gulangyu has a thing for pianos — it's called "Piano Island" for a reason), which has dozens of antique pianos from all over the world. Quirky and charming.

### Getting to Gulangyu

| | |
|---|---|
| **Getting There** | Ferry from **Xiagu Wharf** (Xiamen side) to **Sangong Wharf** (Gulangyu) |
| **Hours** | 7:00 AM — 5:30 PM (scheduled departures every 20 min) |
| **Fee** | ¥35 round-trip / ~$5 (includes return) |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#ferry` `#essential`

**Pro tip:** Book your ferry ticket online in advance through WeChat (search "厦门轮渡" mini-program). The tickets sell out fast on weekends and holidays, and waiting at the terminal is no fun. Also, tourists depart from **Xiagu Wharf** (东渡邮轮中心厦鼓码头), not the one right next to Zhongshan Road. That one's for locals only. I learned this the hard way, so you don't have to.

## Siming District — Where the City Lives

Back on the main island, this is Xiamen's downtown core — where the temples, universities, shopping streets, and food stalls all cluster together. Everything here is walkable or a short cab ride away.

### Nanputuo Temple

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | 515 Siming South Rd, Siming District |
| **Getting There** | Bus 1, 21, 45 to Nanputuo Station; or 20-min walk from Xiamen University |
| **Hours** | 3:00 AM — 7:00 PM (yes, 3 AM — monks start early!) |
| **Fee** | **Free** |
| **Rating** | ★★★★★ |

**Tags:** `#free` `#temple` `#photography` `#spiritual`

![Nanputuo Temple built into the mountainside with ornate Buddhist architecture](/images/xiamen/xiamen_nanputuo_temple_mountain_buddhist_architecture_001.webp)

This was genuinely one of my favorite spots in all of Xiamen. Nanputuo is a working Buddhist temple complex that's been here since the Tang Dynasty, and it's built right into the base of a hill — so the halls just keep going up and up, each one higher than the last. The architecture is stunning: colorful eaves, intricate carvings, and this constant haze of incense smoke that makes everything look dreamy.

What I loved most though was the atmosphere. It's not a tourist trap — local people genuinely come here to pray, light incense, and make offerings. There's a free tea station near the entrance where volunteers hand you a tiny cup of oolong and just smile. And the hill behind the main hall leads up to a viewpoint where you can see the whole temple and the sea beyond.

**Don't miss:** The vegetarian restaurant on the temple grounds. ¥20 for a plate of surprisingly delicious Buddhist tofu and veggies. No meat, all flavor.

### Xiamen University

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | 422 Siming South Rd, Siming District |
| **Getting There** | Right next to Nanputuo Temple — just walk |
| **Hours** | Weekdays 12:00 PM — 2:00 PM (limited hours for visitors); Weekends & holidays all day |
| **Fee** | **Free** (bring ID/passport for entry) |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#free` `#campus` `#photography` `#architecture`

Often called the most beautiful campus in China, and honestly, it's hard to argue. XMU has this incredible mix of traditional Fujianese architecture (white walls, red roofs, upturned eaves) and tropical gardens, all set against a backdrop of green hills. The main draw is **Furong Lake** — a picturesque pond with a black swan or two gliding around — and the avenue of old banyan trees that look like they've been there for centuries.

It's a bit tricky to get in on weekdays since they limit visitor numbers, but on weekends you can just walk in. Go early before it gets crowded, grab a coffee from one of the tiny campus cafes, and pretend you're a student for an hour. The vibe is immaculate.

### Zhongshan Road

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | Zhongshan Rd, Siming District (pedestrian street from Xiamen University area to the waterfront) |
| **Getting There** | 10-min walk from Xiamen University; Metro Line 1 to Zhongshan Park Station |
| **Hours** | Shops open ~10:00 AM — 10:00 PM; street always accessible |
| **Fee** | **Free** (your wallet will not be free) |
| **Rating** | ★★★☆☆ |

**Tags:** `#shopping` `#food` `#nightlife` `#pedestrian_street`

Zhongshan Road is Xiamen's main shopping drag — an arched pedestrian street lined with shops, food stalls, and the occasional colonial-era building peeking through the modern storefronts. It's touristy, yes. Will you find the city's best-kept secrets here? Probably not. But it's a fun walk, especially at night when the neon lights come on and the snack stalls fire up.

The side alleys are where the real action is — duck into any small lane and you'll find tiny food carts selling everything from grilled squid on a stick to the city's famous oyster omelettes. More on those below.

## Beyond the City Center

### Railway Cultural Park

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | North side of Wenyuan Rd, Siming District |
| **Getting There** | Bus to Wenyuan Rd Station; about 15 min from Zhongshan Road by cab |
| **Hours** | Open 24 hours (park), indoor exhibitions ~9:00 AM — 6:00 PM |
| **Fee** | **Free** |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#free` `#photography` `#walking` `#history`

![Railway Cultural Park with old railway tracks and green tunnel](/images/xiamen/xiamen_railway_cultural_park_track_tunnel_green_001.webp)

This one surprised me. It's an old railway line that used to run from the port into the mountains, and now it's been turned into a long, narrow park. The tracks are still there, embedded in the ground, and the whole path is shaded by a canopy of old trees that forms a natural green tunnel. It's about 4 km long, flat, and perfect for a lazy afternoon stroll or a bike ride.

There are little info boards along the way about the history of the railway, a few old train cars you can climb into for photos, and plenty of benches where locals sit and play cards or practice instruments. It's not a "must-see" in the tourist sense, but it's one of those places that makes you feel like you're seeing the real Xiamen — not the one on Instagram.

### Guanyinshan Beach

| | |
|---|---|
| **Address** | Huandao Rd, near Guanyinshan Business District |
| **Getting There** | Bus 68, 58 to Guanyinshan Station; 20-min cab from city center |
| **Hours** | Always open (swim at your own risk outside lifeguard hours) |
| **Fee** | **Free** |
| **Rating** | ★★★☆☆ |

**Tags:** `#free` `#beach` `#sunset` `#relaxation`

![Guanyinshan Beach coastline with waves rolling onto the sand](/images/xiamen/xiamen_guanyinshan_beach_coastline_sea_waves_001.webp)

Look, Xiamen's beaches aren't going to win any awards against Thailand or the Philippines. The water is a bit murky, the sand is more grey than white, and on weekends it gets packed. But Guanyinshan is probably the best of the bunch — wide, clean-ish, and with a nice boardwalk for walking or cycling.

The real reason to come here? **Sunset.** The beach faces west, and when the sky turns orange over the sea with the Xiamen skyline silhouetted on one side... that's a moment. Bring a drink, find a spot on the sand, and just watch. The city noise fades away and you remember — oh yeah, this is an island.

## What to Eat in Xiamen

Okay, this is where things get serious. Xiamen's food scene is a glorious mashup of Fujianese coastal cooking, Taiwanese street food influences, and Southeast Asian flavors from the region's trading history. Everything is fresh, fragrant, and packed with umami.

### Oyster Omelette (蚝仔煎 / Háo Zǎi Jiān)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Where to Try** | **Lianhua Hai Li Jian** (莲花海蛎煎), 318 Lianhua North Rd — the locals' pick |
| **Price** | ¥15–20 / ~$2–3 per plate |
| **Rating** | ★★★★★ |

**Tags:** `#street_food` `#must_try` `#seafood`

This is THE Xiamen street food classic, and it's nothing like the version you might have had in other parts of China. Fresh small oysters are mixed into a sweet potato starch batter with eggs and pan-fried until crispy on the edges and gooey in the middle. It's served with a sweet chili sauce and a sprinkle of cilantro. The texture is... divisive. Some people find the gooey starch off-putting. I find it absolutely addictive. Try it at least once. If you don't like it, fine — but you have to try it.

### Satay Noodles (沙茶面 / Shā Chá Miàn)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Where to Try** | **Wushan Satay Noodles** (乌糖沙茶面), 37-101 Wushan Rd — legendary spot |
| **Price** | ¥20–35 / ~$3–5 per bowl |
| **Rating** | ★★★★★ |

**Tags:** `#noodles` `#soup` `#local_favorite` `#must_try`

This is the dish I still dream about. Satay noodles are a Xiamen specialty — chewy wheat noodles in a rich, nutty, slightly spicy broth made from peanut butter, chili, dried shrimp, and about a dozen other secret ingredients. You pick your toppings (thinly sliced pork, squid, tofu puffs, fish balls, pig blood cake if you're adventurous) and they ladle the hot broth over everything.

Every local has a strong opinion about which shop makes the best version. The one I tried, **Wushan**, has been around for decades and only opens until they run out of soup (usually by 1 PM). Go early. Go hungry. Thank me later.

### Shrimp Rolls (虾卷 / Xiā Juǎn)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Where to Try** | **Ah Jie Shrimp Roll** (阿杰虾卷), Zhongshan Road side alley near the waterfront |
| **Price** | ¥10–15 / ~$1.50–2 per roll |
| **Rating** | ★★★★☆ |

**Tags:** `#street_food` `#snack` `#cheap_eats`

Think of these as Xiamen's answer to spring rolls, but way better. Minced shrimp, pork, and water chestnuts are wrapped in a thin tofu skin and deep-fried until golden and crispy. Dip them in sweet chili sauce or a splash of Xiamen's local dark vinegar. They're small, cheap, and dangerously easy to eat six of without noticing.

## Quick Practical Bits

### Best Time to Visit

October to December and March to May — the weather is mild (18–25°C), the humidity is low, and the tourist crowds are manageable. Summer (June–September) is hot, humid, and typhoon season. Winter (January–February) is cool but still pleasant, just bring a light jacket.

### Getting Around

| Method | Best For | Cost |
|--------|----------|------|
| **Metro** | Long distances (airport, train station, far neighborhoods) | ¥2–7 / ~$0.30–1 |
| **Bus** | Anywhere the metro doesn't go | ¥1–2 / ~$0.15–0.30 |
| **DiDi (Uber)** | When you're tired or in a group | ¥15–40 / ~$2–6 |
| **Bicycle** | Exploring the island, especially Huandao Rd | ¥1–3 / ~$0.15–0.45 per 30 min (shared bike) |
| **Walking** | Siming District — everything is close together | Free |

### Where to Stay

| Area | Vibe | Best For |
|------|------|----------|
| **Near Zhongshan Road** | Central, lively, lots of food | First-time visitors who want convenience |
| **Near Xiamen University** | Quiet, beautiful, near Nanputuo | Budget travelers and solo backpackers |
| **Gulangyu Island** | Romantic, car-free, atmospheric | Couples and anyone who wants a unique experience (book far in advance) |

### What to Bring

- **Comfortable walking shoes** — your feet will thank you on Gulangyu
- **Sunscreen** — even in winter the UV can be strong
- **A light jacket or scarf** — the sea breeze gets chilly at night
- **Cash** — many small food stalls don't take cards or Alipay
- **Your passport** — needed for some temple entrances and ferry tickets

## Final Thoughts

Xiamen is one of those rare cities that actually feels like a vacation, not just a change of scenery for work. It's relaxed without being boring, touristy without feeling fake, and small enough that you never feel overwhelmed but big enough that you keep discovering new corners. Gulangyu alone is worth the trip — I spent two days there and still felt like I barely scratched the surface.

If you've got 3–4 days, you can see the highlights without rushing. If you've got a week, you can actually slow down, eat your way through every street, find your favorite corner of the island, and leave feeling genuinely rested.

Which, let's be honest, is the whole point of a trip anyway, isn't it?

*Go. Eat an oyster omelette. Get lost on Gulangyu. Watch the sunset from the beach. You'll thank yourself.*
