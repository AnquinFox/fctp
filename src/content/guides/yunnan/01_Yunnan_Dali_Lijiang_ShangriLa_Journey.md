##The Ancient Town of Lijiang



> "Lijiang is not a city. It is a memory that happens to have walls."

>Local saying, not entirely wrong



Lijiang Old Town (Dayan) was built by the Naxi people starting in the Song Dynasty (960-1279). Unlike most Chinese cities that follow grid patterns, Lijiang's layout is organic?winding canals, stone bridges, streets that follow water rather than geometry. The Naxi believed water was sacred, and they built their homes around it rather than through it.



![Lijiang Old Town canal with traditional Naxi architecture](/images/yunnan/yunnan_lijiang_old_town_canal_mountains_001.webp)



In 1997, UNESCO declared Lijiang a World Heritage Site. In the years since, it's become one of China's most popular tourist destinations?and it shows. The main streets of Dayan are now a river of souvenir shops, bars playing pop music, and groups of selfie-stick-waving tourists.



**But the magic hasn't disappeared. It has retreated.**



Three blocks off the main street, you'll find quiet lanes where Naxi grandmothers still tend vegetable patches behind wooden gates, where the only sound is water flowing through stone channels. The trick is to get lost?deliberately. Turn down any alley that looks too narrow to fit two people, and you'll find the real Lijiang.

### Jade Dragon Snow Mountain



![Jade Dragon Snow Mountain peak with clouds](/images/yunnan/yunnan_jade_dragon_snow_peak_clouds_003.webp)



Five hundred metres above Lijiang, at a height of 5,596 metres, sits Yulong (Jade Dragon) Snow Mountain. The Naxi believe it is the dwelling place of their protector god, Sanduo. On clear days it dominates the skyline like a frozen wave.



The cable car to **Spruce Meadow** (3,200m) costs 楼180. The one to **Glacier Park** (4,680m) costs 楼280 and sells out hours in advance. If you're not acclimatized to altitude, go to Spruce Meadow?the views are just as good and you won't spend the next day with a splitting headache.



>?**Note on Altitude:** Lijiang sits at 2,400m. Most visitors from sea level will feel breathless climbing stairs on the first day. Take it slow, drink water, and skip the alcohol on arrival night.

##Dali: The Backpacker's Dream



Two hours south of Lijiang by train (楼34, 2 hours), Dali is what Lijiang was 20 years ago: an ancient walled city at the foot of Cangshan Mountain, with Erhai Lake shimmering in the distance.

![Dali Erhai Lake with boats and distant mountains](/images/yunnan/yunnan_dali_erhai_lake_boat_scenic_008.webp)




![Erhai Lake with mountains and reflection](/images/yunnan/yunnan_dali_erhai_lake_mountains_scenic_002.webp)



Dali was the capital of the Bai Kingdom (937-1253), a Buddhist kingdom that controlled the tea-horse trade route between Yunnan and Tibet. The Bai people still live here, recognizable by their white-and-blue embroidered clothing and triple-eaved roofs.



### The Three Pagodas



![Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple with mountain backdrop](/images/yunnan/yunnan_dali_three_pagodas_temple_mountain_004.webp)



Built in the 9th and 10th centuries, these three pagodas are Dali's most iconic sight. The central one, 69 metres tall, has survived a dozen earthquakes in 1,200 years. The reflection pond in front creates the classic photo.

##Shaxi: The Town That Time Forgot



![Shaxi ancient town with temple and market square](/images/yunnan/yunnan_shaxi_ancient_town_temple_market_005.webp)



Shaxi, 2 hours north of Dali, is a restored 2,000-year-old trading post on the Tea Horse Road?the ancient caravan route that connected Yunnan to Tibet and eventually to India.



The Saturday Market here is the most authentic thing I saw in Yunnan. Naxi, Bai, and Yi farmers come down from the mountains to trade horses, herbs, and livestock. No souvenirs, no Instagram spots?just mud, noise, and life.

##Shangri-La & Ganden Sumtseling Monastery



![Ganden Sumtseling Monastery Tibetan temple against mountain](/images/yunnan/yunnan_shangrila_ganden_temple_tibetan_006.webp)



Shangri-La (formerly Zhongdian) is a Tibetan town at 3,400 metres altitude. The name was taken from James Hilton's 1933 novel *Lost Horizon*, describing a mythical Himalayan paradise. The Chinese government officially renamed it in 2001 as a tourism move?and it worked.



The real treasure is **Ganden Sumtseling Monastery**, the largest Tibetan Buddhist monastery in Yunnan. Built in 1681, it houses 700 monks and is often called the "Little Potala Palace." The golden roofs against the grey Tibetan sky are unforgettable.

##Lugu Lake: The Kingdom of Women



![Lugu Lake with boardwalk and mountain reflection](/images/yunnan/yunnan_lugu_lake_boardwalk_mountain_water_007.webp)



On the border between Yunnan and Sichuan, Lugu Lake is home to the Mosuo people?one of the world's last matriarchal societies. Women run the households, make the decisions, and pass property through the female line. They practice "walking marriage"?couples don't live together; the man visits at night, and children stay with the mother's family.
