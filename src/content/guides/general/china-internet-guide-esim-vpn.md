## Why Internet Access in China Is Different

China has its own version of the internet — often called the "Great Firewall" (GFW). It blocks Google, Gmail, WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, Telegram, and many other Western services.

> **You cannot just land in China, buy a local SIM, and expect Google Maps to work.** It won't.

The firewall got a major upgrade in April 2026 (more on that later), which broke many traditional VPN solutions. But don't worry — there are two solid paths forward, and I'll walk you through both.

## Part 1: eSIM — The Easiest Solution ⭐

If you only need **mobile data** (not a Chinese phone number), a travel eSIM is by far the simplest option. Here's why I recommend this as your primary solution:

- **No installation needed** — activate before you leave, it starts working when you land
- **International routing** — your data exits China through Hong Kong or Singapore, naturally bypassing the firewall
- **Dual-SIM ready** — keep your home SIM active for calls/texts while using the eSIM for data

The catch: your phone needs to support eSIM. Most iPhones (XR and newer), Samsung flagships (S20+ and newer), and Google Pixels do. If your phone doesn't support eSIM, skip to Part 2.

## Part 2: VPN — For Local SIM Users

If you need a **Chinese phone number** (for DiDi, Meituan, bank accounts, etc.), you'll need a local SIM. And with a local SIM comes the need for a VPN.

VPNs are also essential if:
- Your phone doesn't support eSIM
- You're staying long-term (months, not weeks)
- You need faster, more reliable access for work

### ⚠️ The April 2026 GFW Upgrade Changed Everything

This is the most important thing to understand about VPNs in China right now:

**What happened:**
- **March 2026:** Hubei police started fining individuals ¥200-500 for using VPNs (targeted at Chinese citizens)
- **Early April 2026:** The GFW physically removed thousands of relay servers inside China. This was a hard blow — protocols like Shadowsocks, V2Ray, and Trojan that relied on these intermediate servers were **largely crippled overnight**
- **April 28, 2026:** LetsVPN (快连), one of the most popular VPNs for China, permanently exited the Chinese market
- **May-July 2026:** Users shifted to a "primary + backup" dual-VPN strategy

**The result:** Many VPNs that worked in 2025 no longer work. The ones that survived either have proprietary protocols or were specifically designed to handle China's unique network conditions.

## Part 3: Practical Tips

After a few trips and more than a few headaches, here's what I've learned:

### Before You Leave

- **Install everything before you go.** VPN websites are blocked in China. Download the app, create your account, and test it while you're still home.
- **Bring a backup.** I always travel with two options — an eSIM for data and a VPN for when I need to connect through a local network. If one fails, the other saves the day.
- **Take screenshots.** Screenshot your eSIM activation QR code and VPN setup instructions. You won't always have data to look things up.

### What to Expect

- **VPN speeds will be slower** than what you're used to at home. Budget for 10-30% overhead. It's fine for messaging and web browsing, but streaming at 4K might struggle.
- **Peak hours matter.** VPN connections tend to be slower during Chinese evening hours (8-11 PM). If you have urgent work, plan around it.
- **Some apps work without a VPN.** WeChat, Alipay, DiDi — these are Chinese apps that work fine on a local network. Don't waste VPN bandwidth on them.
- **Hotel Wi-Fi is not a substitute.** Many hotels block VPN connections entirely. Your phone data plan is more reliable.

### Common Mistakes

- **Don't use free VPNs.** They don't work, and some are security risks.
- **Don't wait until you arrive.** The Great Firewall doesn't care about your travel plans.
- **Don't assume your VPN from last year still works.** The situation changes constantly. Check recent reports before each trip.

### Legal Note

China's VPN regulations primarily target Chinese citizens and commercial activities. Foreign tourists are, in practice, at very low risk. That said, keep a low profile — use your VPN for communication and information, not for political activities, and you'll be fine.
